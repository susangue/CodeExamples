Strategic plans provide consistency across parks that are managing resources amid change. These strategies are applicable to all science-based decision making, resource, and park planning actions. You will find links to these documents in the Reference document on the Resources tab.

When examining strategic plans, think about the following questions:

  • How do they address anticipated or unexpected changes in resource conditions?
  • Do they allow the NPS flexibility to respond to change? If so, how much latitude is given?
  • Do they utilize the concept of adaptive management?

Select each book to learn about examples of strategic planning documents.

National Park Service Natural Resource Stewardship and Science Framework (2016)

In Topic 1 we briefly touched on this framework that identifies four pillars that guide the NPS to adapt and respond to continuous change. Each pillar includes focus areas.

The focus areas for managing amid continuous change include:

  • Promote Restoration that Builds Long-term Adaptive Capacity
  • Understand and Develop Mitigation Measures for Resolving Stressors Related to Land-Use Change
  • Plan for and Respond to Climate Change
  • Anticipate and Address Emerging Issues
  • Understand and Respond to Changing Socioeconomic Characteristics and Cultural Values that are Relevant to the NPS Mission

Learn more about this framework by reading the document on the Resources tab.

Science Communication Framework (2016)

Science communication is a core function of park science and resource stewardship employees, particularly in an era of rapid change. This framework provides a guide to help a wide variety of users develop science communication products, practices, programs, and structures.

Guiding principles include:

  • Presentation of accurate and current knowledge
  • Fostering learning
  • The use of the best communications practices

National Park Service Climate Change Response Strategy (2010)

The NPS Climate Change Response Strategy provides direction to address the impacts of climate change. Its goals and objectives fall under four integrated components:

  • Science: Collaborate with partners to identify and monitor climate change effects in parks and to apply accurate and relevant science to management and policy decisions
  • Adaptation: Develop feasible and actionable scenarios and create a flexible framework for dealing with impacts
  • Mitigation: Reduce the National Park Service’s carbon footprint through energy-efficient and sustainable practices and integrate these practices into planning and op­erations
  • Communication: Raise employees’ and the public’s awareness of the implications of climate change and inspire them to take steps to address this challenge

National Park Service Coastal Adaptations Strategy Handbook (2016)

This handbook summarizes the current state of NPS climate adaptation and key approaches currently in practice or considered for climate change adaptation in coastal areas. The handbook:

  • Identifies tools and strategies
  • Provides examples of approaches that the agency and individual parks are using to address coastal vulnerabilities and climate change impacts
  • Provides policy and decision-making guidelines

Other Federal, State, and Local Documents

Depending on your job and the resources you are working with, you may need to be aware of other strategic planning documents such as:

  • State air quality plans
  • State comprehensive and issue-specific wildlife plans (e.g. chronic wasting disease, hunting regulations on adjacent or state land)
  • Interagency exotic and pest management plans (e.g. gypsy moth, emerald ash borer, bark beetles)
  • National Park Service issue decision documents (deer management, winter use, chronic wasting disease, etc.)

These types of documents may influence NPS decisions and planning. Being aware of plans that extend beyond park boundaries can foster better coordination, collaboration and anticipation of future changing conditions.
